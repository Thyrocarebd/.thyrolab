Important information for ELISA

Principle of ELISA

The principle of ELISA is based on the binding of an antigen (Ag) to its specific antibody (Ab). The antigen or antibody is immobilized on a solid surface (usually a 96-well microtiter plate), and the detection of this antigen-antibody complex is amplified using an enzyme that produces a measurable color change in the presence of its substrate.

Types of ELISA

Direct ELISA

In direct ELISA, an antigen is adsorbed onto a solid surface, and a labeled primary antibody (conjugated with an enzyme) is added to bind the antigen. After washing away unbound antibodies, a substrate is added, and the enzyme converts the substrate into a colored product.
Example: Detection of viral proteins in a sample.

Indirect ELISA

This involves the use of an unlabeled primary antibody specific to the antigen and a labeled secondary antibody (conjugated to an enzyme) that binds to the primary antibody. The secondary antibody amplifies the signal and leads to more sensitive detection.
Example: Measurement of antibodies in blood (e.g., HIV antibody testing).

Sandwich ELISA

In this type, a "capture" antibody is first coated onto the plate, and the antigen of interest is added and bound by this antibody. A second antibody (detection antibody) binds to another epitope of the antigen. This forms a "sandwich" of antibody-antigen-antibody, which is then detected using a labeled secondary antibody or directly if the detection antibody is labeled.
Example: Detection of cytokines (e.g., IL-6) in serum.

Competitive ELISA

In competitive ELISA, the sample antigen competes with a labeled antigen for binding to the primary antibody. The decrease in signal (due to reduced binding of labeled antigen) correlates with the amount of antigen in the sample.
Example: Detection of small molecules like hormones or drugs (e.g., testosterone levels).

Uses of ELISA

Medical diagnostics: Used to diagnose infections (e.g., HIV, Hepatitis B, COVID-19), autoimmune diseases, and allergies.
Research: For detecting and quantifying proteins, peptides, hormones, and antibodies in biological samples.
Food industry: Detection of food allergens (e.g., peanuts, gluten).
Environmental science: Detection of contaminants (e.g., pesticides) in water or soil samples.

Steps in ELISA Procedure

Coating: A known antigen or antibody is adsorbed onto a microplate well.
Blocking: Unbound sites are blocked with a protein solution (e.g., BSA or skim milk) to prevent non-specific binding.
Incubation: The sample containing the antigen or antibody is added to the well and incubated for binding.
Washing: Excess unbound molecules are washed away using a buffer.
Detection: The enzyme-conjugated antibody is added for detection, followed by another washing step.
Substrate addition: A substrate is added that reacts with the enzyme to produce a color change.
Reading: The intensity of the color change is measured using a spectrophotometer.

Instructions for Better Results During Manual Testing

Proper Plate Coating: Ensure uniform coating of antigen or antibody by using optimized concentrations and following the incubation times recommended by the protocol.
Efficient Washing: Ensure thorough washing to remove unbound antibodies or reagents, as inadequate washing can lead to high background and false-positive results.
Avoid Contamination: Use fresh pipette tips and wear gloves to avoid sample contamination.
Incubation Conditions: Maintain proper incubation temperature and time. Variations can affect antibody binding and enzyme activity.
Substrate Handling: Avoid exposure of substrates to light before use, as some are light-sensitive. Use fresh substrate solutions to avoid compromised reactions.
Control Wells: Always include positive and negative controls to validate the assay performance.
Minimize Edge Effects: Use a humidified chamber for plate incubation to prevent evaporation at the edges, which can cause uneven results.
